Answer 1: The steam engine had a monumental impact on the transportation of goods during the Industrial Revolution. Before James Watt's improvements, moving heavy items like coal and textiles was slow and expensive, relying almost entirely on horse-drawn wagons or canals which were limited by terrain and weather. The adoption of high-pressure steam engines into locomotives allowed for the creation of the railway system. This meant that massive quantities of raw materials could be transported from mines directly to factories in a fraction of the time. This efficiency lowered the cost of production and allowed for the mass distribution of finished products across the country, fundamentally changing the scale of the global economy.

Answer 2: The shift from rural to urban living, often called urbanization, was driven by the changing economic landscape. For centuries, the majority of the population lived in small, rural farming communities where they were self-sufficient. However, as new farming technologies reduced the need for labor in the fields and the new factories in the cities required thousands of workers, people began to migrate en masse. Cities like Manchester and Birmingham grew rapidly because they provided the employment opportunities that rural areas no longer could. This migration was not just about changing locations; it was a total transformation of life, moving people away from the seasonal rhythms of farming to the constant activity of the industrial city.

Answer 3: The factory system completely revolutionized the nature of labor for common workers by moving production away from the traditional 'cottage industry' model. In the old system, craftsmen worked from their own homes, setting their own hours and completing every stage of a product's creation. In the factory system, labor became centralized and highly regimented. Workers had to follow rigid clocks and bells, working for 12 to 14 hours a day on a single, specialized task. This 'division of labor' meant that workers no longer needed to be master craftsmen; they just needed to perform one specific action repeatedly. While this increased efficiency, it also made the work much more monotonous and dangerous, and it stripped workers of the autonomy they once had in their own homes.
